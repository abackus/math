The paper I'll be reading will be Terry Tao's ``Sendov's conjecture for sufficiently high degree polynomials." This paper looks interesting to me because it applies ``cheap nonstandard" methods to prove a result in complex analysis. In addition it uses probability-theoretic methods, which I'm learning a lot of right now. Sendov's conjecture is the following:

Let $latex {f: \CC \rightarrow \CC}&fg=000000$ be a polynomial of degree $latex {n \geq 2}&fg=000000$ that has all zeroes $latex {\leq 1}&fg=000000$. If $latex {a}&fg=000000$ is a zero then $latex {f'}&fg=000000$ has a zero in $latex {\overline{D(a, 1)}}&fg=000000$.

Tao notes that by Tarski's theorem, in principle it suffices to get an upper bound $latex {n_0}&fg=000000$ on the degree of a counterexample and then use a computer-assisted proof to complete the argument. I think that for every $latex {n \leq n_0}&fg=000000$ you'd get a formula in the theory of real closed fields that could be decided in $latex {O(n^r)}&fg=000000$ time, where $latex {r}&fg=000000$ is an absolute constant (which, unfortunately, is exponential in the number of variables of the formula, and so is probably quite large). Still, something to keep in mind -- computer-assisted proofs seem like the future in analysis.

More precisely, Tao proves the following:

Proposition 1 For every $latex {n}&fg=000000$ in a monotone sequence in $latex {\NN}&fg=000000$, let $latex {f}&fg=000000$ be a monic polynomial of degree $latex {n}&fg=000000$ with all zeroes in $latex {\overline{D(0, 1)}}&fg=000000$ and let $latex {a \in [0, 1]}&fg=000000$ satisfy $latex {f(a) = 0}&fg=000000$. It's now pretty natural to see how ``cheap nonstandard" methods apply. One can pass to a subsequence countably many times and still preserve the hypotheses of the proposition, so by diagonalization and compactness, we may assume good (but ineffective) convergence properties. For example, we can assume that $latex {a = a^{(\infty)} + o(1)}&fg=000000$, where $latex {a^{(\infty)}}&fg=000000$ does not depend on $latex {n}&fg=000000$ and $latex {o(1)}&fg=000000$ is with respect to $latex {n}&fg=000000$.

So one can view the proposition model-theoretically: it says that if $latex {n}&fg=000000$ is a nonstandard natural number, $latex {f}&fg=000000$ a monic polynomial of degree $latex {n}&fg=000000$ with a zero $latex {a \in [0, 1]}&fg=000000$, and there are no zeroes of $latex {f'}&fg=000000$ in $latex {\overline{D(a, 1)}}&fg=000000$, then $latex {0 = 1}&fg=000000$. Tao never fully takes this POV, but frequently appeals to results like the following:

Henceforth we will use asymptotic notation in the nonstandard sense; for example, a quantity is $latex {o(1)}&fg=000000$ if it is infinitesimal. This is equivalent to the cheap nonstandard perspective where a quantity is $latex {o(1)}&fg=000000$ iff it is with respect to $latex {n}&fg=000000$, where $latex {n}&fg=000000$ is ranging over some monotone sequence of naturals. I think the model-theoretic perspective is helpful here because we are going to pass to subsequences a lot, and at least in the presence of the boolean prime ideal theorem, letting $latex {n}&fg=000000$ be a fixed nonstandard natural number is equivalent to choosing a nonprincipal ultrafilter on $latex {\NN}&fg=000000$ that picks out the subsequences we are going to pass to, in the perspective where $latex {n}&fg=000000$ ranges over a monotone sequence of standard naturals.

This follows because the Stone-Cech compactification $latex {\beta \NN}&fg=000000$ is exactly the space of ultrafilters on $latex {\NN}&fg=000000$. Indeed, if $latex {n}&fg=000000$ ranges over a monotone sequence of standard naturals, then in $latex {\beta \NN}&fg=000000$, $latex {n}&fg=000000$ converges to an element $latex {U}&fg=000000$ of $latex {\beta \NN \setminus \NN}&fg=000000$, which then is a nonprincipal ultrafilter. If $latex {\NN^\omega/U}&fg=000000$ denotes the ultrapower of $latex {\NN}&fg=000000$ with respect to $latex {U}&fg=000000$, then I think the equivalence class of the sequence $latex {\{1, 2, \dots\}}&fg=000000$ in $latex {\NN^\omega/U}&fg=000000$ is exactly the limit of $latex {n}&fg=000000$. Conversely, once a nonprincipal ultrafilter $latex {U \in \beta \NN \setminus \NN}&fg=000000$ has been fixed, we have a canonical way to pass to subsequences: only pass to a subsequence which converges to $latex {U}&fg=000000$. This is possible since $latex {\beta \NN}&fg=000000$ is compact.

I think it will be at times convenient to go back to the ``monotone sequence of standard naturals" perspective, especially when we're doing computations, so I reserve the right to go between the two. We'll call the monotone sequence perspective the ``cheap perspective" and the model-theoretic perspective the ``expensive perspective".

Let $latex {\zeta}&fg=000000$ be a random zero of $latex {f'}&fg=000000$, drawn uniformly from the finite set of zeroes. Le $latex {\lambda}&fg=000000$ denote a random zero of $latex {f}&fg=000000$ chosen independently of $latex {\zeta}&fg=000000$.

Okay, it's pretty obvious what being infinitesimally close to a deterministic standard real is in the uniform or probabilistic sense. Expanding out the definition of the vague topology of measures, a nonstandard measure $latex {\mu}&fg=000000$ on a locally compact Hausdorff space $latex {Q}&fg=000000$ is infinitesimally close to a standard measure $latex {\nu}&fg=000000$ provided that for every continuous function $latex {f}&fg=000000$ with compact support,

$latex \displaystyle \left|\int_Q f~d\mu - \int_Q f~d\nu\right| = o(1).&fg=000000$

This induces a definition of being infinitesimally close in distribution.

$latex \displaystyle U_\eta(z) = \EE \log \frac{1}{|z - \zeta|}.&fg=000000$

Here $latex {\EE}&fg=000000$ denotes expected value. Tao claims but does not prove that this definition makes sense for almost every $latex {z \in \CC}&fg=000000$. Clearly the latter claim implies the former. To check this, let $latex {K}&fg=000000$ be a compact set $latex {\subseteq \CC}&fg=000000$ equipped with Lebesgue measure $latex {\mu}&fg=000000$ and let $latex {\nu}&fg=000000$ be the distribution of $latex {\zeta}&fg=000000$. $latex \displaystyle \int_K U_\eta(z) ~d\mu(z) = \int_K \int_\CC \log \frac{1}{|z - \omega|} ~d\nu(\omega) ~d\mu(z)&fg=000000$

and the integrand is singular along the set $latex {\{z = \omega\}}&fg=000000$, which has real codimension $latex {2}&fg=000000$ in $latex {K \times \supp \nu}&fg=000000$. $latex \displaystyle s_\eta(z) = \EE \frac{1}{z - \eta}.&fg=000000$
