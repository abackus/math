Lemma 3 For every $latex {1 < |z| \leq 3/2}&fg=000000$, we have

$latex \displaystyle s_\lambda(z) = \mathbf E \frac{1}{z - \lambda}&fg=000000$

$latex \displaystyle \log |s_\lambda(z)| \in \left[\log \frac{1}{|z| + 1}, \log \frac{1}{|z| - 1}\right].&fg=000000$

$latex \displaystyle U_\lambda(z) - \frac{n - 1}{n} U_\zeta(z) = \frac{1}{n} \log |s_\lambda(z)|&fg=000000$

$latex \displaystyle -\frac{\log(|z| + 1)}{n} \leq U_\lambda(z) - \frac{n - 1}{n}U_\zeta(z) \leq -\frac{\log(|z| - 1)}{n}&fg=000000$

$latex \displaystyle U_\lambda(z) - \frac{n - 1}{n} U_\zeta(z) = O\left(\frac{1}{n} \log \frac{1}{|z|-1}\right)&fg=000000$

hence

$latex \displaystyle U_\lambda(z) - U_\zeta(z) = O\left(\frac{1}{n} U_\zeta(z) + \frac{1}{n} \log \frac{1}{|z|-1}\right).&fg=000000$

Since $latex {|\zeta| < 1}&fg=000000$ while $latex {1 < |z| < 3/2}&fg=000000$, $latex {|z - \zeta|}&fg=000000$ is bounded from above and below by a constant times $latex {|z| - 1}&fg=000000$. To derive the second claim from the first, we use the previous lemma, which implies that we must show that

$latex \displaystyle \log \frac{1}{|z| - 1} = O(n)&fg=000000$

$latex \displaystyle U_\eta(Re^{i\theta}) = -\log R + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{m \neq 0} \frac{e^{im\theta}}{|m| R^{|m|}} \mathbf E\eta^{|m|}.&fg=000000$

$latex \displaystyle \log \frac{1}{|Re^{i\theta} - w|} = -\log R + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{m \neq 0} \frac{e^{im\theta} w^{|m|}}{|m| R^{|m|}}.&fg=000000$

and the imaginary parts all cancel by symmetry about $latex {0}&fg=000000$. Using the symmetry about $latex {0}&fg=000000$ again we get

$latex \displaystyle -\log R + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{m \neq 0} \frac{e^{im\theta} w^{|m|}}{|m| R^{|m|}} = \sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{w^{|m|} \cos |m|\theta}{|m|}.&fg=000000$

Lemma 5 For all $latex {m \geq 1}&fg=000000$, one has

$latex \displaystyle \mathbf E\lambda^m - \mathbf E\zeta^m = O\left(\frac{m \log m}{n}\right).&fg=000000$

$latex \displaystyle \frac{e^{im\theta}}{|m| R^{|m|}} \mathbf E(\lambda^{|m|} - \zeta^{|m|}) = O\left(\frac{1}{n} \log \frac{1}{R - 1}\right).&fg=000000$

$latex \displaystyle \mathbf E\lambda^m - \mathbf E\zeta^m = O\left(\frac{m R^m}{n} \log \frac{1}{R - 1}\right)&fg=000000$

Here I was puzzled for a bit. Surely if two random variables have the same moment-generating function then they are identically distributed! But, while we can define the moment-generating function of a random variable as a formal power series $latex {F}&fg=000000$, it is not true that $latex {F}&fg=000000$ has to have a positive radius of convergence, in which case the inverse Laplace transform of $latex {F}&fg=000000$ is ill-defined. Worse, the circle is not simply connected, and in case one, we have to look at a uniform distribution on the circle, whose moments therefore aren't going to points on the circle, so the moment-generating function doesn't tell us much.

2. Balayage

We recall the definition of the Poisson kernel $latex {P}&fg=000000$:

$latex \displaystyle P(Re^{i\theta}, re^{i\alpha}) = \sum_{m = -\infty}^\infty \frac{r^{|m|}}{R^{|m|}} e^{im(\theta - \alpha)}&fg=000000$

whenever $latex {0 < r < R}&fg=000000$ is a radius. Convolving the Poisson kernel against a continuous function $latex {g}&fg=000000$ on $latex {\partial B(0, R)}&fg=000000$ solves the Dirichlet problem of $latex {B(0, R)}&fg=000000$ with boundary data $latex {g}&fg=000000$.

Definition 6 Let $latex {\eta \in D(0, R)}&fg=000000$ be a random variable. The balayage of $latex {\eta}&fg=000000$ is

$latex \displaystyle \text{Bal}(\eta)(Re^{i\theta}) = \mathbf EP(Re^{i\theta}, \eta).&fg=000000$

Balayage is a puzzling notion. First, the name refers to a hair-care technique, which is kind of unhelpful. According to Tao, we're supposed to interpret balayage as follows.

If $latex {w_0 \in B(0, R)}&fg=000000$ is an initial datum for Brownian motion $latex {w}&fg=000000$, then $latex {P(Re^{i\theta}, w_0)}&fg=000000$ is the probability density of the first location $latex {Re^{i\theta}}&fg=000000$ where $latex {w}&fg=000000$ passes through $latex {\partial B(0, R)}&fg=000000$. Tao asserts this without proof, but conveniently, this was a problem in my PDE class last semester. The idea is to approximate $latex {\mathbf R^2}&fg=000000$ by the lattice $latex {L_\varepsilon = \varepsilon \mathbf Z^2}&fg=000000$, which we view as a graph where each vertex has degree $latex {4}&fg=000000$, with one edge to each of the vertices directly above, below, left, and right of it. Then the Laplacian on $latex {\mathbf R^2}&fg=000000$ is approximated by the graph Laplacian on $latex {L_\varepsilon}&fg=000000$, and Brownian motion is approximated by the discrete-time stochastic process wherein a particle starts at the vertex that best approximates $latex {w_0}&fg=000000$ and at each stage has a $latex {1/4}&fg=000000$ chance of moving to each of the vertices adjacent to its current position.

So suppose that $latex {w_0}&fg=000000$ and $latex {Re^{i\theta}}&fg=000000$ are actually vertices of $latex {L_\varepsilon}&fg=000000$. The probability density $latex {P_\varepsilon(Re^{i\theta}, w_0)}&fg=000000$ is harmonic in $latex {w_0}&fg=000000$ with respect to the graph Laplacian since it is the mean of $latex {P_\varepsilon(Re^{i\theta}, w)}&fg=000000$ as $latex {w}&fg=000000$ ranges over the adjacent vertices to $latex {w_0}&fg=000000$; therefore it remains harmonic as we take $latex {\varepsilon \rightarrow 0}&fg=000000$. The boundary conditions follow similarly.

Now $latex {\eta}&fg=000000$ if is a random initial datum for Brownian motion which starts in $latex {D(0, R)}&fg=000000$, the balayage of $latex {\eta}&fg=000000$ is again a probability density on $latex {\partial B(0, R)}&fg=000000$ that records where one expects the Brownian motion to escape, but this time the initial datum is also random.

I guess the point is that balayage serves as a substitute for the moment-generating function in the event that the latter is just a formal power series. We want to be able to use analytic techniques on the moment-generating function, but we can't, so we just use balayage instead.

Let $latex {\psi}&fg=000000$ be the balayage of $latex {\eta}&fg=000000$. Since $latex {\eta}&fg=000000$ is bounded, we can use Fubini's theorem to commute the expectation with the sum and see that

$latex \displaystyle \psi(Re^{i\theta}) = \sum_{m-\infty}^\infty R^{-|m|} e^{im\theta} \mathbf E(r^{|m|} e^{-im\alpha}) = 1 + 2\sum_{m=1}^\infty R^{-|m|} \cos m\theta \mathbf E(r^{|m|} \cos m\alpha)&fg=000000$

provided that $latex {\eta = re^{i\alpha}}&fg=000000$. It will be convenient to rewrite this in the form

$latex \displaystyle \psi(Re^{i\theta}) = 1 + 2\text{Re} \sum_{m=1}^\infty R^{-m}e^{im\theta} \mathbf E\eta^m&fg=000000$

so $latex {\psi}&fg=000000$ is uniquely determined by the moment-generating function of $latex {\eta}&fg=000000$. In particular, $latex {\lambda^{(\infty)}}&fg=000000$ and $latex {\zeta^{(\infty)}}&fg=000000$ have identical balayage, and one has a bound

$latex \displaystyle \text{Bal}(\lambda)(Re^{i\theta}) - \text{Bal}(\zeta)(Re^{i\theta}) = O\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{m \log m}{R^m}\right).&fg=000000$

We claim that

$latex \displaystyle \sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{m \log m}{R^m} = O\left(-\frac{\log(R-1)}{(R - 1)^2}\right)&fg=000000$

which implies the bound

$latex \displaystyle \text{Bal}(\lambda)(Re^{i\theta}) - \text{Bal}(\zeta)(Re^{i\theta}) = O\left(\frac{1}{n}\frac{\log\frac{1}{R-1}}{(R - 1)^2}\right).&fg=000000$

To see this, we discard the $latex {m = 1}&fg=000000$ term since $latex {1 \log 1 = 0}&fg=000000$, which implies that

$latex \displaystyle \sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{m \log m}{R^m} = \sum_{M=1}^\infty \sum_{m=2^M}^{2^{M+1} - 1} \frac{m \log m}{R^m}.&fg=000000$

Up to a constant factor we may assume that the logarithms are base $latex {2}&fg=000000$ in which case we get a bound

$latex \displaystyle \sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{m \log m}{R^m} \leq C\sum_{M=1}^\infty \frac{M2^M}{R^{2^M}}.&fg=000000$

The constant is absolute since $latex {R \in (1, 3/2]}&fg=000000$.

By the integral test, we get a bound

$latex \displaystyle \sum_{M=1-\log(R-1)}^\infty \frac{M2^M}{R^{2^M}} \leq C\int_{-\log(R-1)}^\infty \frac{x2^x}{R^{2^x}} ~dx \leq C\int_{-\log(R-1)}^\infty \frac{2^{x^{(1+\varepsilon)}}}{R^{2^x}} ~dx.&fg=000000$

Using the bound

$latex \displaystyle \int_{1/(R-1)}^\infty \frac{dy}{R^y} \leq CR^{-1/(R-1)} \leq C2^{-1/(R-1)}&fg=000000$

for any $latex {N}&fg=000000$ and the change of variable $latex {y = 2^x}&fg=000000$ (thus $latex {dy = 2^x \log 2 ~dx}&fg=000000$), we get a bound

$latex \displaystyle \sum_{M=1-\log(R-1)}^\infty \frac{M2^M}{R^{2^M}} \leq C \int_{-\log(R-1)} \frac{dy}{R^y} \leq C2^{-1/(R-1)}&fg=000000$

since the $latex {\varepsilon}&fg=000000$ error in the exponent can't affect the exponential decay of the integral in $latex {1/(R-1)}&fg=000000$. Since we certainly have

$latex \displaystyle 2^{-1/(R-1)} \leq C\frac{-\log(R-1)}{(R-1)^2}&fg=000000$

this is a suitable tail bound.

To complete the proof of the claim we need to bound the main term. To this end we bound

$latex \displaystyle \sum_{M=1}^{-\log(R-1)} \frac{M2^M}{R^{2^M}} \leq \log\frac{1}{R-1} \sup_{x > 0} \frac{x2^x}{R^{2^x}} = \log\frac{1}{R-1} 2 \uparrow \sup_y \frac{y \log y}{R^y}.&fg=000000$

Here $latex {\alpha \uparrow \beta = \alpha^\beta}&fg=000000$ denotes exponentiation. Now if $latex {R - 1}&fg=000000$ is small enough (say $latex {R - 1 < 3/4}&fg=000000$), this supremum will be attained when $latex {x > 1}&fg=000000$, thus $latex {y \log y \leq 2y}&fg=000000$. Therefore

$latex \displaystyle \sum_{M=1}^{-\log(R-1)} \frac{M2^M}{R^{2^M}} \leq \left(2\uparrow \sup_{y > 0} \frac{y}{R^y}\right)^2 \log\frac{1}{R-1} .&fg=000000$

Luckily $latex {yR^{-y}}&fg=000000$ is easy to differentiate: its critical point is $latex {1/y = \log R}&fg=000000$. This gives

$latex \displaystyle \sup_{y > 0} \frac{y}{R^y} \leq \log \frac{1}{R - 1}&fg=000000$

so

$latex \displaystyle \left(2\uparrow \sup_{y > 0} \frac{y}{R^y}\right)^2 \leq \frac{1}{(R-1)^2}&fg=000000$

which was the bound we needed, and proves the claim. Maybe there's an easier way to do this, because Tao says the claim is a trivial consequence of dyadic decomposition.

Let's interpret the bound that we just proved. Well, if the balayage of $latex {\eta}&fg=000000$ is supposed to describe the point on the circle $latex {\partial B(0, R)}&fg=000000$ at which a Brownian motion with random initial datum $latex {\eta}&fg=000000$ escapes, a bound on a difference of two balyages should describe how the trajectories diverge after escaping. In this case, the divergence is infinitesimal, but at different speeds depending on $latex {R}&fg=000000$. As $latex {R \rightarrow 1}&fg=000000$, our infinitesimal divergence gains a positive standard part, while if $latex {R}&fg=000000$ stays close to $latex {3/2}&fg=000000$, the divergence remains infinitesimal. This makes sense, since if we take a bigger circle we forget more and more about the fact that $latex {\zeta,\lambda}&fg=000000$ are not the same random variable, since Brownian motion has more time to ``forget more stuff" as it just wanders around aimlessly. So in the regime where $latex {R}&fg=000000$ is close to $latex {3/2}&fg=000000$, it is reasonable to take standard parts and pass to $latex {\zeta^{(\infty)}}&fg=000000$ and $latex {\lambda^{(\infty)}}&fg=000000$, while in the regime where $latex {R}&fg=000000$ is close to $latex {1}&fg=000000$ this costs us dearly.

3. Case zero

Suppose that $latex {a}&fg=000000$ is infinitesimal.

We showed last time that $latex {\zeta \in \overline{D(0, 1)} \setminus \overline{D(a, 1)}}&fg=000000$, so $latex {d(\zeta, C) = O(a)}&fg=000000$ is infinitesimal. Therefore $latex {\zeta^{(\infty)} \in C}&fg=000000$ almost surely.

I think there's a typo here, because Tao lets $latex {K}&fg=000000$ range over $latex {D(0, 1) \setminus C}&fg=000000$ and considers points $latex {e^{i\theta} \in D(0, 1) \setminus C}&fg=000000$, which don't exist since $latex {|e^{i\theta}| = 1}&fg=000000$ while every point in $latex {D(0, 1)}&fg=000000$ has $latex {|\cdot| < 1}&fg=000000$. I think this can be fixed by taking closures, which is what I do in the next lemma.

Tao proves a ``qualitative" claim and then says that by repeating the argument and looking out for constants you can get a ``quantitative" version which is what he actually needs. I'm just going to prove the quantitative argument straight-up. The idea is that if $latex {K}&fg=000000$ is a compact set which misses $latex {C}&fg=000000$ and $latex {\lambda \in K}&fg=000000$ then a Brownian motion with initial datum $latex {\lambda}&fg=000000$ will probably escape through an arc $latex {J}&fg=000000$ which is close to $latex {K}&fg=000000$, but $latex {J}&fg=000000$ is not close to $latex {C}&fg=000000$ so a Brownian motion which starts at $latex {\zeta}&fg=000000$ will probably not escape through $latex {J}&fg=000000$. Therefore $latex {\lambda,\zeta}&fg=000000$ have very different balayage, even though the difference in their balayage was already shown to be infinitesimal.

I guess this shows the true power of balayage: even though the moment-generating function is ``just" a formal power series, we know that the essential supports of $latex {\lambda,\zeta}&fg=000000$ must ``look like each other" up to rescaling in radius. This still holds in case one, where one of them is a circle and the other is the center of the circle. Either way, you get the same balayage, since whether you start at some point on a circle or you start in the center of the circle, if you're a Brownian motion you will exhibit the same long-term behavior.

In the following lemmata, let $latex {K \subset \overline{D(0, 1)} \setminus C}&fg=000000$ be a compact set. The set $latex {\{\theta \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2): e^{i\theta} \in K\}}&fg=000000$ is compact since it is the preimage of a compact set, so contained a compact interval $latex {I_K \subseteq (-\pi/2, \pi/2)}&fg=000000$.

Lemma 7 One has

$latex \displaystyle \inf_{w \in K} \int_{I_K} P(Re^{i\theta}, w) ~d\theta > 0.&fg=000000$

Proof: Since $latex {K}&fg=000000$ is compact the minimum is attained. Let $latex {w}&fg=000000$ be the minimum. Since $latex {P}&fg=000000$ is a real-valued harmonic function in $latex {w}&fg=000000$, thus

$latex \displaystyle \Delta \int_{I_K} P(Re^{i\theta}, w) ~d\theta = \int_{I_K} \Delta P(Re^{i\theta}, w) ~d\theta = 0,&fg=000000$

the maximum principle implies that the worst case is when $latex {K}&fg=000000$ meets $latex {\partial D(0, R)}&fg=000000$ and $latex {w \in \partial D(0, R)}&fg=000000$, say $latex {w = Re^{i\alpha}}&fg=000000$. Then
